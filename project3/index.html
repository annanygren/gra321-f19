A Quick Look at the Brandenburg Gate

The Brandenburg Gate was built between 1788 and 1791. It was created to be the key entry point to Berlin by Prussian King Frederick William II. The Gate is topped by a statue known as the Quadriga, which is a statue of the goddess of victory driving a chariot pulled by four horses.

A Moment with Napoleon

The statue called Berlin home for a little over a decade before Napoleon Bonaparte and his Grand Army conquered the city in 1806. He ordered the Quadriga to be dismantled and shipped to Paris. But after Napoleon's defeat at the Battle of Waterloo (1815), the Quadriga was proudly reinstated on the Brandenburg Gate and from that point onward, became a symbol of victory; an iron cross/eagle were added to the laurel wreath.

The Brandenburg Gate During WWII and Onward

The Gate was used as a Nazi party symbol as they rose to power. It endured severe damage during the war but was restored in 1957 which included recasting the Quadriga from original molds. Even after WWII the Brandenburg gate had negative associations. From 1961 to 1989, it symbolized division since it shut off access to freely move from East to West.

In December 2000, the Brandenburg Gate was privately refurbished at a whopping 6 Million Euros!! On the 20th anniversary of the Fall of the Wall, the Brandenburg Gate became the main venue for the Festival of Freedom. Presently, the gate is closed to vehicle traffic and much of the surrounding area has turned into a pedestrian zone and popular venue for concerts/gatherings.
